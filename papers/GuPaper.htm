ABSTRACT

Efficient and friendly access to information returned by search engines is needed. Yet, there are significant limitations to the general-purpose Web search engines. These search engines too often return lists of URLs that are inconveniently long, poorly ordered and with many irrelevant elements. These problems often make it prohibitively cumbersome and time consuming to systematically examine the lists, but to realize the full potential of these lists requires just such a systematic examination. The usual imperfect solutions involve improving the lists by ordering them using relevance measuring algorithms, imposing taxonomies as in Yahoo, and various approaches to refining the query.

While real time solutions continue to be important, non-real time solutions will always have the advantage of more available computation time, resulting in potentially better results. Such solutions will always have application in cases where preprocessing time is available, such as during authoring (e.g. of e-books) and in the context of digital libraries.  Our system addresses preprocessing of bodies of text of the order of book size. In particular, we focus on sets of Web documents that are too large to be conveniently browsed without significant browsing assistance, which we supply by a preprocessing strategy that converts a set of Web documents into a novel repository that supports browsing. Such sets of Web documents may be chosen because they are of general interest, so that they may be preprocessed and then made generally available for browsing in real time in a Web browser over the Web; alternatively, sets of documents may be chosen to conform to a particular search engine query or other specification, in cases where the prospective user is willing to wait for the repository to be created in return for improved browsability later.

KEYWORDS: Browser, search engine, text, repository

INTRODUCTION

Current search engines have serious limitations, a major one being poor organization of the returned list of URLs. One way to reduce the number of irrelevant links is to provide a predetermined taxonomy, and search only among documents that are in the desired taxonomic category, as at Yahoo. This is only a partial solution as it is presently labor intensive to create the taxonomy and locks users into a one-size-fits-all taxonomic structure that might not be suitable for a given information seeking need. The standard way to solve the other major problem, excessive length of returned URL lists, is to provide the items in the returned lists in order of their estimated relevance. While relevance ordering is useful, it is well known that the quality of the resulting orderings is imperfect. Even ordering methods that are claimed to be outstanding often have erratic performance on specific searches. Newer approaches to bringing more relevant material to users’ attention include clustering of the documents (e.g. Etzioni and Zamir 1998).

In cases where real time performance is not a requirement, more intensive processing can potentially produce better results. For example, the paragraphs constituting a set of documents can be individually processed. An example of this is OMIM (McKusick et al.), which allows clicking on a paragraph to retrieve related documents. Our system takes this further by allowing clicking on a paragraph to retrieve related paragraphs. Retrieved paragraphs are to be presented along with their containing documents. The system we are building is intended to work as an interface to an ordinary Web search engine rather than as a special purpose, site-specific search engine as in the case of OMIM. Our system also displays retrieved material directly (“direct display”) rather than displaying links to retrieved material (“indirect display”) and displays a montage of several retrieved items at a time (“multidisplay”) instead of one at a time (“monodisplay”).  The remainder of this paper describes:

1.        The step by step processing procedure of our system, MultiBrowser II.

2.        The current status of the implementation.

3.        Technical results regarding the use of n-grams that may also be of interest to others who are using n-grams for text processing.

1 THE USER’S VIEW OF THE SYSTEM

The design of the system is oriented toward facilitating a text display strategy that links each paragraph to several related paragraphs, while simultaneously displaying color-coded relatedness information about the documents containing displayed paragraphs. Since two highly related paragraphs can be in either related or unrelated documents, it is desirable for a text display strategy to represent such situations, and our strategy is designed to meet this constraint. Figure 2 shows a sample display.

As Figure 2 shows, the display window includes six sub-windows (the user can choose the number of sub-windows), which are used to display the content of six documents. There is a color bar and a URL above each sub-window. This URL points to the source of this document; a user can go to the website if interested. The color bar indicates the relatednesses among the different documents whose paragraphs are displayed. The more similar the colors, the more similar the documents.

In the next section we describe the processing involved in comparing documents in our system. In doing so we investigate some relevant aspects of n-gram based representations of texts, as our comparisons use a cosine measure based on n-grams because it combines conceptual simplicity with good results in text comparison tasks (Damashek 1995). Then in the following section we describe the paragraph comparison aspect of the project, though briefly because while it is an important part of the system it is currently unimplemented.

2 document-Scale processing

This step involves downloading a set of documents and clustering them, using the K-Means clustering algorithm, into three clusters. These clusters are labeled with the primary colors red, green and blue. Then each document is assigned a color consisting of a combination of the primary colors, with the amount of each color determined by the distance of the document from the centroids of the three clusters. Finally, this color is displayed whenever content from the document is displayed, as a colored document tag (Figure 2), to give the reader a visual sense of how this document compares to other documents in the display. This provides a context for the focus, which is the visible displayed part of the document, thereby using the well-known focus+context strategy.

The document processing system has been implemented as a working prototype. The rest of this section provides details about the document processing. The next section investigates some technical aspects of our n-gram based analysis that are also of more general interest to n-gram based text analysis.

A: Downloading the documents

The system starts by accessing a standard Web search engine, giving it a query, and downloading the documents in the list of URLs returned by the search engine. Eventually we plan to handle up to 30 mbytes of text. Thirty mbytes of text is typical for about 500 Web documents (see appendix). This downloading, and subsequent processing, takes time. Users could let it run while doing other things or overnight, or librarians could use it to create browsable repositories which, once created, would then be available for interactive browsing in real time.

B: Clustering the documents

Once downloaded, the documents are clustered. (Later, the paragraphs will be subjected to an independent clustering step which will directly support navigation from a paragraph to related paragraphs.) Each of the display windows will be labeled with a color derived from the relationships between the document displayed therein and the cluster centroids (Figure 2), to further inform users.

To cluster the documents, every document is transformed into a corresponding vector of n-grams, for n=5 (Damashek 1995). More precisely, the vectors contain hash codes of n-grams, to exploit the sparseness of the space of 5-grams in order to save space. The vectors encode the number of times each n-gram hash code appears in the corresponding document. These vectors are the input to the clustering algorithm.

To cluster the space of document vectors, we first normalize the vectors so that they are the same length. After that, all documents (represented by the heads of vectors of n-gram hash codes) fall on the hypersurface of a hypersphere in the many-dimensional space whose axes are the hash codes in the space of possible hash codes (Figure 1). A standard clustering algorithm, K-Means, is used to partition the document vectors into three clusters, based on their proximity to each other.

C: Generating Color Codes for the Documents

For each document, the system generates an RGB color based on the corresponding distances from the head of the document vector to the centroids of clusters R, G, and B. This color is made the background color of a small HTML file to be used as a colored tag for the corresponding document. When a document is displayed, the displayed portion will start at the boundary of some paragraph in it, and the color tag of the document will be displayed in a horizontal bar above the frame containing the document. Thus documents that are measured to be similar will be displayed with similarly colored horizontal bars, allowing readers to quickly and visually determine relatedness among documents displayed simultaneously in different sub-window frames (Figure 2). Each document’s horizontal bar also contains the URL from which the document was downloaded, increasing the information density of the color tag.

 

3 USING N-GRAMS FOR DOCUMENT COMPARISON

In order to cluster the documents, we first convert each document into a vector of 5-grams. Documents are then compared by comparing their associated n-gram vectors. This has been shown to provide good results while economizing on computation (Damashek 1995). Ideally, each component of such a vector represents the number of occurrences of a given n-gram in the corresponding document. However, because of the large number of n-grams that can occur and their sparseness in the even larger combinatorial space of conceivable n-grams, to save space the n-grams are hashed and the vectors are actually vectors of hash codes. These vectors support a cosine-based document similarity metric (Damashek 1995). For example, if all possible 5-grams of ASCII numbers 0 - 127 are considered, there are 1285=235=34359738368 possibilities. However, Damashek (1995) recommends a hash code size of 18 bits (corresponding to a prime table size slightly lower than 218) as one that provides a good tradeoff between minimizing both hash table size and collision frequency. Collisions are simply ignored, in that in some number of cases, 2 (or more) different n-grams will hash to the same location, which will then be used to record the summed number of occurrences of all the n-grams hashing to that location. Collisions therefore reduce the quality of the vector representation of the document to some degree, and thus should be minimized.

 

We note first that the frequency of collisions, while generally decreasing with increasing (prime) table size, as expected, does not do so monotonically. There is considerable "noise" in this curve that can have a significant effect on collision rates. We investigated this for 5-grams (the size recommended by Damashek) and used a sample of 100 different documents to control for noise due to chance characteristics of particular documents. Figure 3 shows that collision frequency generally decreases with increasing table size but has the aforesaid noise. 4 PARAGRAPH PROCESSING


Each paragraph in each document will be provided with an icon which, when clicked, executes a CGI (common gateway interface) program that retrieves the six most closely related paragraphs in the set of documents and sends to the user’s browser their containing documents, specified to display with the initial focus of each sub-window frame at the beginning of the corresponding retrieved paragraph, as in Figure 7. To find the paragraphs most related to any given paragraph, the same n-gram based vectorization process followed for whole documents will be used. Each paragraph will have a “More” button at the end. Clicking on one of the white, rectangular “More” buttons (see Figure 7) will retrieve the next 6-paragraph display.

5. Related work

Document clustering continues to be investigated as a technique for organizing documents retrieved by search engines on the Web. The intent is to try to group documents together based on their similarities. Given good clusters, users should be able to find interesting documents more easily than otherwise, as well as understand the structure of the body of retrieved documents better.

 

Visualization techniques can assist users in dealing with document clusters efficiently. One class of visualizations groups documents sharing such attributes as size, publication date, forum, etc. Another class compares documents based on their content of keywords, n-grams, etc. The present work uses a simple visualization abstraction of a document – a color – to allow convenient visual perception of the relatedness among documents which have passages presented and visible on the screen.

 

The present work uses n-grams for document comparison and hence for clustering. Damashek (1995) describes using n-grams for document comparison. Damashek explicitly ignores collisions, a matter that we investigate further here. Cohen (1997) focuses on recursive hashing of n-grams as a way to improve computation speed, while the present work focuses on hashing issues relevant to memory usage.

 

Document clustering has traditionally been performed in batch mode, but recently the need to cluster results returned by search engines in real time has led to work on real time clustering. Such clustering methods process documents relatively superficially since deeper analyses cannot be done in real time. For example, Excite (www.excite.com) identifies potentially useful search terms for refining the search, based on their ability to define a cluster among the returned documents, and hence to weed out other documents. The NorthernLight search engine (www.northernlight.com) provides "Custom Folders" and the retrieved documents are organized in these folders. These folders are clusters based on several parameters of the documents, such as type, source, language or subject. 

 

Grouper (Zamir and Etzioni 1998) works as a meta-search engine interface. It queries different search engines, and only looks at the top hits from each. Thus, its clusters are not affected by many low relevance documents, as is the case in NorthernLight’s folders. It also can run on a client machine, making it a friendly component of distributed IR systems. It is also argued to be scalable. Grouper relies on the concept of coherent clusters, meaning that the clustering algorithm groups similar documents together without excluding a document from some reasonable cluster just because it is already in another cluster. Thus Grouper can generate overlapping clusters. Grouper uses Suffix Tree Clustering (STC) – a fast, incremental, linear time clustering algorithm that produces coherent clusters. Better known in the bioinformatics field, suffix trees have a variety of computational advantages, and are argued to provide results in Grouper that are both faster and more precise than previous algorithms.

 

Future work

We plan to investigate the non-monotonicity of the relationship between hash table size and collision rate in the context of n-grams in more detail. One result will be an empirical accounting of what table sizes are best given an approximate (power-of-two) target size for a range of target sizes.

We also plan to complete the browsing system by implementing the second half, namely the paragraph-scale processing portion.

Conclusion

This paper discusses the relation between collisions and the hash keys in the n-gram method. It shows an appropriate hash key can be chosen to get better performance. Then it presents a novel strategy for assisting users to browse the URLs returned by Web search engines. It uses paragraph-level processing to provide fine-grained matching of related topics, while providing color tags encoding the subject of the containing documents. Display is via a direct multidisplay HCI strategy to facilitate efficient interaction with texts over the Web.
