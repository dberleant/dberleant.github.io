1 Motivation

Improved efficiency of interaction between humans and documents is an important problem that continues to be addressed on several fronts. Finding the right documents has been addressed historically by library card catalogs, more recently by on-line and CD-based bibliographic search services, and most visibly by World Wide Web search engines. Extensive research in information retrieval has addressed finding documents, using both text processing and user interface strategies. User interface strategies have in recent years emphasized diverse visualization techniques that help in navigating in a space containing large numbers of documents (####citations). Once documents have been identified as relevant, efficient interaction with them enables efficient location and absorption of information in them of interest to the reader. Historically such interaction has been supported by tables of contents, indexes, suitable writing style and good document organization. More recent forms of support are enabled by storing documents digitally, and include full text search, hypertext and hypermedia, and user interfaces (### citations) to support efficient and friendly interaction with documents. Such user interfaces rely on the fact that users of digital documents should be supported by tools that help them locate embedded passages, since readers of on-line text tend to scan and pick out passages rather than reading in a traditional sense (Nielsen 1999). Immediate applications include on-line manuals, dealing with collections of dozens of Web documents on a topic, and e-books. 

2 Multibrowser Interfaces

A multibrowser interface contains adjoining, non-overlapping subwindows. Each subwindow provides a view of the document or some ancillary information, collectively providing a more complex view of the document (Figure 1). By presenting a user with multiple information items simultaneously without requiring the user to request each one individually, the intent is to provide information in a user-friendly way that promotes its absorption, with minimal distraction from the interface. Interactive means of updating the displayed view provides a mechanism for browsing, so that each individual view constitutes customized information. Customized information addresses the needs of a particular user at a particular moment (###Berghel and Berleant and Berleant and Berghel (both parts)). 

Such an interface must be interactive, and facilities for recalling previous views, specifying new information needs, and requesting new views will typically be present. In the interface depicted in Figure 1 for example, text in the subwindow, when clicked, causes a new view to be generated and displayed. 

Figure 1. Sample output of a direct multidisplay prototype, running at http://engr.uark.edu/~djb/R/NLP/IC/MultiBrowser/versions/Zhou/infproto.html.

Interfaces such as that of Figure 1 exemplify both direct display and multidisplay. The following definitions help explain these characteristics.

Definition: multidisplay is a collage of non-overlapping items without spaces between them. 

Collage-based computer displays (Kaltenbach et al. 1991; Kerne 1997; Yeung and Yeo 1997; Yeo and Yeung 1997; Wittenburg et al. 1998; Wittenburg et al. 1999###use numbers instead?Add the other references, or just the numbers, see the lit. review in a later section) show multiple items simultaneously. While items are usually overlapping or have spaces between them, in the video domain multidisplay is used in Video Posters (Yeung and Yeo 1997; Yeo and Yeung 1997 ###use numbers instead?). An everyday example of multidisplay is the newspaper, since different articles, advertisements, etc., are presented on the same page. The visual modality is clearly more suited to multidisplay than the auditory modality, since it is hard to pick out and attend to a stream of speech or other audio stimulus amid several such streams that are simultaneously present. On the other hand, taste and smell provide a direct multidisplay to the brain because we can perceive more than one taste and smell simultaneously (adding greatly to the dining experience).

Definition: monodisplay is the presentation of one item.

Typical information retrieval systems, once they display content from a document, use monodisplay. For example, Web search engines respond to a query with a list of URLs, and the user can easily display only one of those items at a time.

Definition: direct display is the presentation of actual content.

An example of direct display is presentation of text from a document. Direct display is contrasted with indirect display.

Definition: indirect display is the presentation of meta-content.

By meta-content we mean information about content rather than content itself. An example is the list of URLs returned by a search engine or, more generally, the lists of references returned by bibliographic search systems such as on-line card catalogs, search interfaces to digital libraries such as the ACM digital library (www.acm.org/dl/), and so on. These systems provide such meta-content as titles, authors, and brief extracts whose main purpose is seemingly to help the reader decide whether to access the full document itself.

Definition: direct multidisplay is presentation of material in a way that conforms to both direct display and multidisplay. 

Note that indirect multidisplay, direct monodisplay, and indirect monodisplay may be defined similarly. We compare direct multidisplay to the alternatives next.

 2.1 Comparison: Multidisplay and Monodisplay

The ideal interface is one which is unnoticed, reading the mind of the user and from that successfully determining and presenting the optimal information. This ideal is not obtainable, at least not in the near future, but interfaces that move the state of the art in the direction of the ideal represent progress, if not perfection. Multidisplays can do better than monodisplays in this regard, under the appropriate circumstances.

When the user needs a particular, identified item of information, a monodisplay of that item is appropriate. However when there are more than one candidate items, multidisplay has advantages over monodisplay. The fundamental reason for these advantages center around the issue of reducing cognitive overhead during information seeking. The two main ways in which multidisplay can reduce this cognitive overhead compared to monodisplay are in reducing time spent on interface actions, and reducing attention required to matters other than the information itself. Let's look at these in more detail.

Time. User interface motor actions such as moving and clicking a mouse take time, and multidisplay addresses the consequent need to reduce the number of such actions by having one interface action lead to several responses (Berghel et al. 1999). In the case of monodisplay, at least one interface action is required to bring up each item viewed. In many applications a pair of such actions will be needed, one to back up to a choice point, and another to navigate forward to a new information item. Common Web search engines exemplify the two action case, as they require backing up to the returned URL list before examining the next URL in that list. In contrast, multidisplay allows one interface action to bring up a number of items to view (Figure 2), eliminating the need for a separate interface action or pair of interface actions to view each item, and thereby reducing the total number of interface actions. 

Attention. The more user attention used in dealing with the interface, the less is available to use in dealing with the presented information itself. Every user interface action represents not only an expenditure of time, but also an interruption in the user's attention to the main task, processing presented information. Additional attention used in keeping track of the navigation environment represents an additional cognitive overhead. An example of the need for such attention is in skimming the content of each item in a list of items (for example, skimming each URL in a list of URLs returned by a Web search engine). While attending to the skimming itself, the user must also maintain a mental navigation model consisting of a tree whose root is the list and whose leaves are the items, and which is navigated in one direction by a "go back" operation and in the other by a link click, and further, some way of flagging items that have already been visited so that they are not visited again inadvertently, and perhaps some way of remembering which visited items had valuable information and which didn't. These requirements reduce the amount of mental energy available to deal with the information in the items.

Individual subwindows could also be of different sizes, perhaps due to different estimated importances of their content, as in "pictorial summaries" (Yeo and Yeung 1997, Yeung and Yeo 1997).

2.2 Comparison: direct display and indirect display

While direct display involves presenting actual content, indirect display involves presenting meta-content that takes the place of actual content, abstracting it in some way. Familiar indirect displays include icons that expand into computer applications when clicked, and descriptively labeled hypertext links. Note that icons and links do not necessarily meet the definition of indirect displays* - they must communicate meta-content. This abstracting property implies the advantages and disadvantages that direct display has compared with indirect display. These advantages and disadvantages are shown in Table 1.

2.3 The Four Display Approaches: Applicability of Each

If we consider display approaches based on whether they use multidisplay or monodisplay, and orthogonally whether they use direct or indirect display, there are four possible combinations. Each is best suited to certain application types.

Indirect monodisplay. Good for showing an icon representing an application program not in use but needing to be conveniently invoked.

Direct monodisplay. Good when the user wishes to interact with a given information item (e.g. when using a word processor or reading a particular Web document).

Indirect multidisplay. Good when there are many competing information items, so that some abstraction is needed to show them all at once in a limited amount of space (e.g. visual navigation tools for use with large numbers of documents).

Direct multidisplay. Good when there are several competing things to display, so that there is room to display all of them simultaneously. This paper focuses on direct multidisplay for text browsing.

3 Multibrowsers: Text Browsing With Direct Multidisplay

We have been exploring direct multidisplay for text browsing, and call direct multidisplay based browsers multibrowsers. Multibrowsers display multiple extracts of a document simultaneously. Navigation actions can result in a new display of  multiple extracts. This simple application of direct multidisplay could be useful in such everyday contexts as on-line help systems and Web search engine result presentation, as well as cutting edge applications such as electronic books. We have built a number of prototype systems that illustrate some of the variations in functionality and diverse architectures that are possible. These systems are described in this section.

3.1 A Standalone Multibrowser

The first multibrowser (Potti 1996) was a standalone system written in Visual Basic (Figure 3). The subwindows vary in background color so that they can be distinguished from one another without expending screen real estate on borders for that purpose. It featured a vertical set of five document summarizing subwindows on the left, and another vertical set of three retrieval subwindows on the right. All subwindows have approximately the same text display area, and scroll bars appear if the displayed passage is too long to fit.

Figure 3. Screen shot of the Standalone Multibrowser.

The document summarizing subwindows each hold a sentence from the document, collectively providing a brief summary centered around the sentence best matching the user query. That sentence is displayed in the middle of the five left hand subwindows. The others hold sentences preceding and succeeding that one, as shown in Figure 4. 

Figure 4. Algorithms for filling the subwindows in the Standalone Multibrowser. The variable n-gram algorithm generates n-grams of different lengths from different length words, setting n to 70% of the length of a word under consideration, rounded down. 

As figure 4 shows, the three subwindows on the right hand side display three sentences retrieved from the document using three different algorithms. This was for robustness, and tends to ensure that good matches are displayed even if a particular algorithm fails to perform well. While our experience was that the algorithms generally retrieved reasonable passages, they also often retrieved different passages (as in Figure 3).

This Standalone Multibrowser features forward and backward buttons to navigate among the screens that the browser has displayed during a session. This capability has been found to be valuable in browsers (###). The font button simply controls the font of the display (the input document is a plain text file).

The interface of this multibrowser interface has limitations that, in hindsight, seem obvious. These include the display of only one sentence per subwindow, and the non-networked nature of the system which requires the displayed document to be resident on the machine. These and other limitations are explored further as the paper proceeds.

3.2 A Web Client Multibrowser

The Web client multibrowser (Mu 1997) provided a direct multidisplay interface to documents, like the standalone multibrowser just described, but was written in Java and explored some additional functionalities and design characteristics. The major differences follow.

  • Unlike the standalone multibrowser it can read documents over the Web in addition to from the local drives.
  • It has vertically oriented buttons directly to the right of each major display subwindow that, when clicked, use the sentence in the corresponding subwindow as the next query (Figure 5).
  • It can take as input either a single URL typed into the typein window at the top of Figure 5, or can be given a list of URLs. These may be typed into the large empty subwindow on the left in Figure 5, or the list may be stored in a file and the name of the file typed into the small typein window just above the large empty subwindow. When handling multiple URLs, it responds to queries by extracting sentences from anywhere in the downloaded set.
  • Its main retrieval algorithm is user settable to be either word based, n-gram based with constant n, or n-gram based with n calculated from the length of the word from which n-grams are being extracted (via the checkbox group in the lower left of Figure 5), while a single best matching sentence may be retrieved for any of the 3 algorithms at any time by clicking the corresponding search button at the lower right corner of Figure 5.
  • The value of n for the constant n-gram algorithm is user settable, and for the variable n-gram algorithm, the fraction of a word length used to generate n-grams from it is user settable (lower left of Figure 5).
  • A display mode toggle invokes an alternative display mode that allows the entire document to be displayed in a large subwindow for reading (Figure 6).

Like the standalone multibrowser, various limitations are evident (after the multibrowsers are reviewed, Table 1 will summarize their major characteristics).

3.3 A CGI Multibrowser

A self-contained program operating as a standalone or client system has as disadvantage that anyone wanting to use it must find, download, and install it, and then it will only work on certain platforms (or in the case of Java applications, Java must also be installed). Then, if later versions become available they must be installed to be used, making software maintenance issues more visible than is optimal. An alternative that avoids all these problems is for a program to be run at a remote site and its output served to the user's computer. We have built two such multibrowsers. One is termed the CGI (Common Gateway Interface) multibrowser (Zhou 1997) because it uses the CGI Web protocol (###citation), and the other is termed the server add-on multibrowser (Huang 1998) because it is implemented as a module of the Apache Web server program. The CGI multibrowser is reviewed in this section, and the server add-on multibrowser in the next. 

Figure 1 shows sample output of the CGI multibrowser. The multibrowser software and data files are stored at the server site and called by the server when a remote Web browser such as Netscape or Internet Explorer requests the multibrowser's URL. The system responds to a user query by providing to the client an HTML file specifying a set of frames to be displayed. Each frame contains the entire document that is being browsed, but since the document is normally too big to fit within the frame, each frame displays the document starting at a point in it chosen to be relevant to the query. Since the frames have scroll bars, it is easy to scroll around in the document using whichever frame contains the passage that best catches the reader's attention. 

To implement this capability, each sentence in the document is automatically provided with a distinct HTML anchor (using the HTML <a name="anchorName"> command). Then each frame determines the location in the document it will initially focus on via a URL specification giving not only the document but the anchor as well (for example, a frame specifying the URL someFile.html#sentence47 will display someFile.html starting at the location in it of anchor sentence47). 

In addition to having its own anchor, each sentence is also a clickable hyperlink. When the user clicks on some sentence in some frame, a CGI (Common Gateway Interface) program runs at the server site. This program considers the clicked sentence to be a query, identifies related sentences in the document, and dynamically constructs HTML source specifying a set of frames, each displaying the document with an initial focus on one of those sentences. The HTML source is returned to the remote client Web browser which displays the frames to the user as in Figure 1.  

3.4 A Server Add-on Multibrowser

This multibrowser (Huang 1998), like the CGI multibrowser uses HTML frames to display. This multibrowser is implemented as a C++ module that is compiled as part of the Apache server (Figure 7). Its characteristics include the following.

  • Words in the subwindows that match words in the query are highlighted and clickable. Clicking one expands the subwindow as shown in Figure 8.
  • Clicking on an arrow icon uses the following sentence as the next query. This leaves links embedded by the document author in the sentence unaffected, which would not be the case of the sentence itself was made clickable.
  • Like for the CGI multibrowser, the entire document is in each subwindow, providing context for the retrieved sentence. The user can scroll to see that context.

3.5 A Web Alcove Multibrowser

A commonly cited deficiency of Web search engines is that they often return lists of URLs that are poorly ordered and consequently hard to browse. Consequently, users tend to make suboptimal use of the resources represented by URLs that are returned, particularly those past the first 10 or so entries in the returned list. These documents may contain relevant information that goes unnoticed because of the poor structure of the returned information and the lack of support for browsing it. We are developing a system that will convert such a list of resources into another form, intended to support browsing of the information in those resources via a direct multidisplay interface approach. Figure 9 illustrates the look and feel of the system.

###Figure 9. Six subwindows each display material starting at a retrieved paragraph from the document set. The user can scroll to the other parts of those documents with the scroll bars. Above each subwindow is a colored horizontal bar whose components of red, green, and blue encode the nearness of the document whose paragraph is displayed to the centroids of 3 document clusters in the set of documents. Thus the three frames with similarly colored bars rendered as dark gray in the b&w figure contain documents related to stacks as a data structure; the two frames with similarly colored almost black horizontal bars contain documents related to stacks as piles of things; and the one frame with a light gray bar contains a document related to the "MCGV Stack" organization. The bars supplement retrieved paragraphs with information about the relatedness of their containing documents.

To navigate, the user clicks the "M o r e" icon immediately following a paragraph. This causes the frames containing the next 6 paragraphs to display. Thus the "M o r e" icon constitutes a one-to-many hyperlink. These paragraphs are those which are the most related to the paragraph associated with the clicked "M o r e" icon. Relatedness is determined by applying the cosine metric to the locations of paragraphs in the many-dimensional space of 5-grams (Damashek 1995).

3.6 A Multibrowser Based Web Search Engine Interface

Web search engines have significant shortcomings, as previously noted. Although their relevance ordering function is frequently mentioned as such a shortcoming, that is not the only one. Another is that query terms may be buried within the URL so that simply clicking to obtain the URL is not enough to assess it; one must scroll around in it or even use the Web browser's string search utility, perhaps only to ultimately discover that the document has changed and no longer even satisfies the query.

The Google search engine addresses this problem in that, when returning a list of URLs, it provides a short passage from each that contains a query term (unlike most search engines which provide a short passage from the beginning, regardless of the query). But even Google's keyword in context (kwic) approach is imperfect, because sometimes this small amount of text is not enough for the user to assess the URL without downloading and looking at it, and that in turn leads to the same scrolling and string search needs as with other search engines. We have written a prototype multibrowser that addresses this problem in a way that is complementary to Google's approach.

Our approach uses a split window technique resulting in two subwindows (Berleant et al. 2000). The document is simply split into two parts, with the split occurring just prior to the first occurrence of a query term in the document. The two parts are each displayed in a separate frame, with the query term occurrence shown at the top of one of the frames. See Figure 10.

This has a number of advantages. One is simply that a query term occurrence is easily seen, eliminating the need for scrolling and string searches to find it. Another is that protection from getting "lost" in the document while scrolling around in it is provided, because the split point is so easily found, by scrolling the upper frame all the way down or the lower frame all the way up. Yet another advantage is that the split point depends on the query, providing an information customization function (Berleant and Berghel, 1994). Finally, in focusing on the part of the document containing the query while displaying its position in the document (via the relative lengths of the scroll bars of the two subwindows) the display exhibits the desirable focus+context property (### focus+context citations?).

Figure 10. The query term was "rigorousness." Alta Vista returned a list of URLs, and when the user clicked on one of them, it was downloaded by our proxy server, split, and sent on to the client to be displayed as shown. Note that the lower subwindow begins with the sentence containing the term "rigorousness." The upper subwindow, if scrolled downward, would end at the point in the original document where the lower subwindow begins.

4.1 Navigation Issues for Multibrowsers

To be both useful and usable, queries to a multibrowser system should be both flexible and convenient. Unfortunately these tend to conflict, in that the expressiveness that provides flexibility in querying comes at the price of overhead on the part of the user who must input the query. To achieve both high flexibility and high convenience, two query methods can be provided, one high in flexibility (but correspondingly low in convenience) and one high in convenience (but correspondingly low in flexibility). We have done that in the CGI and Web server add-on multibrowsers (Sections 3.4 and 3.5) and are doing it in the Web alcove multibrowser (section 3.5) by providing type-in boxes for flexible input of queries formulated as text (words and phrases), while providing clickable hyperlinks that act as convenient queries. The objective in designing click-to-execute queries is to make them as likely as possible to be queries that the user would find useful. We do this by making a link retrieve passages that are related to the passage associated with the link. Compared to retrieval of one related passage, the retrieval of a number of such passages in separate subwindows greatly increases the probability of at least one passage of interest being retrieved, as well as increasing the probability of more than one passage of interest being retrieved from zero to quite possible.

Navigation includes not only querying, but screen history navigation. History navigation is an important part of how people interact with web browsers (Catledge and Pitkow 1995; Tauscher and Greenberg 1997). Therefore this is part of the designs of all our multibrowser systems, and is particularly easy to include in systems that incorporate standard Web browsers because such browsers have history navigation capabilities built in.

Next we discuss related work, first on interfaces, next on information retrieval, and last but not least on paradigms for user interaction with text.

4.2 Related Work on Interfaces for Browsing Hypermedia

Multidisplays are collages (Kaltenbach and Frasson 1989), about which significant work has been reported. The NoteCards system (Halasz 1988) could display several information items on the screen at once, although the term "collage" for such a display seems to have originated in Kaltenbach and Frasson (1989) which describes a display consisting of several individual information items in the context of presenting mathematical proofs. They later expand its application to hypertext (Kaltenbach et al. 1991). Since then, the collage display strategy has inspired a number of systems that emphasize display of images. Yeo and Yeung (1997) describe a system that implements a number of collage-based presentation schemes, all focused on supporting their digital video database management system (VDBMS), including support for efficient browsing of videos. Kerne's (1997) CollageMachine constructs collages over a period of time, either interactively or acting independently, using material from Web pages that are crawlable from a root URL provided by the user. These collages can be used as a kind of browser, or viewed as art. Komlodi and Marchionini (1998) found that collages consisting of storyboards of frames from a video were more effective than serial presentation of the same frames for the task of correctly identifying objects depicted in the frames; their experiments also suggest (below the level of statistical significance) that on a variety of measures collages are often but not always more effective than serial presentation. In related work Tse et al. (1998) found that users preferred key frames  presented as storyboards###one word or two?Propagate### as compared to serially, while performance assessment results were "mixed." Wittenburg et al. (1998) describe a Java prototype  that presents collages of images taken from Web pages as previews of those pages. The collage is thus a compact display from which the user can make efficient navigation choices. This is used as part of the SeeHere video browsing system (Wittenburg et al. 1999). Collages can also be the output of an authoring system, such as a system to help users create Web pages consisting in part of pieces of other Web pages (octopus.com 2000, Valds 2000).

The present work focuses on collages without overlaps or spaces among constituent items (multidisplays) of Web documents, which typically have a large text component. While much previous work on collages has dealt primarily with non-textual information presented with overlaps or spaces, some work has involved collages of text without overlaps or spaces. Halasz (1988) and Kaltenbach et al. (1991) focus on text-based information although there are spaces###check that. Kerne's CollageMachine (1997) produces collages that contain text passages in addition to images from the URLs it extracts from, but overlaps occur###check that.  The "pictorial summaries" described in Yeo and Yeung (1997) and Yeung and Yeo (1997) to support efficient video browsing present images rather than text, but do use a multidisplay technique. Like the present work, Kandogan and Schneiderman's (1997) Elastic Windows is a text oriented technique that uses direct multidisplay and hence could be classified as a multibrowser interface technique. The sophisticated approach to window resizing in Elastic Windows might be useful in future multibrowser systems.

4.3 Related Work on Information Retrieval

Because presentation of text (and more generally, hypermedia) in subwindows requires identification of the proper text to present, information retrieval and related topics constitute an important body of related work. For identifying passages from within documents, rather than identifying documents from larger sets of documents as in traditional information retrieval, documents need to be segmented into passages which are then matched against queries. Documents may be parsed into passages based on document structure (e.g. sentence or paragraph boundaries), arbitrary length criteria, or automatically detected topic or theme shifts (cite PNNL paper on wavelets###). We have used sentences as the text unit but are currently (Section 3.5) working with paragraph-length passages. Kaszkiel and Zobel (1997) review much of the passage retrieval literature; see also Miller et al. (1998) and Salton et al. (1994, 1996). Whether passages or entire documents, many techniques useful in identifying text to present have been reported, as illustrated by the following list.

Keyword vectors, boolean expressions on keywords, weighted boolean expressions, cosine measures (with pivoting and without), automatic term weighting based on document and background frequencies, stemming, term truncation, wildcards, thesaurus lookup and other lexicon lookups, sentence based retrieval, passage based retrieval (e.g. Callan 1994), paragraph based retrieval, word sense disambiguation based on context (e.g. Berleant 1995), part of speech tagging of passages, part of speech tagging of natural language queries, partial parsing (e.g. Yangarber and Grishman 1997), phrasal analysis of questions (Kupiec 1993), relevance feedback, n-gram based term comparisons (e.g. Damashek 1995), natural language query processing, automatic extracting, abstracting, and summarizing (###better reference needed Kupiec et al. 1995), question answering (e.g. Burke et al. 1997; Pilkington 1992; Richardson), flexible query answering (e.g. Andreasen et al. 1998), keychaining (Berghel, Berleant, Foy, & McGuire 1999), selective traversal (Salton et al. 1994), visualization of keyword collocations (Hearst 1995; Gershon and Eick 1995), automatic index generation (e.g. Symbolics 1985), iterative query refinement (citation? Kostoff?###), suffix trees (cite Etzioni?###), ranking algorithms, search term generalization, automatic table of contents generation, and more.

Length considerations prevent reviewing all of the possible techniques, but particularly noteworthy ones from the list above are reviewed briefly below. Many of these reviews focus on their use in current Web search engines because of the search engines have high visibility, define the de facto state of the practice for lay information retrieval users in a rapidly evolving and competitive business environment, and probably need to eventually evolve into sophisticated reader interaction systems, the focus of the present work. 

Automatic Abstracting and Extracting. This is relevant to reader interaction with texts because an automatically generated abstract or extract of a document could be displayed in one of the subwindows of a direct multidisplay, providing a summary in that subwindow that could be a useful component of the overall view. Such a summary could be either a general purpose abstract or a customized (Berleant and Berghel 199?###) extract generated in response to the user's query.  Automatic summarization is one of the oldest topics in automatic text processing (Luhn 1958) and has continued to draw extensive attention (e.g. Mani 1998). As Salton et al. (1996) put it, summarization can support ``a type of speed reading where an area of interest is specified, and the best text passages representing that area are chosen in response.'' We describe real time interactive summarization of this type in Berghel et al. (1999). Automatic summarization is not (yet) supported by Web search engines although when google.com returns a list of URLs, each is annotated with a short passage from it containing a query term.

Natural language queries. User friendly reader text browsing seems to require both easy and flexible requests for passages (Section 4.1). We have done this by designing multibrowsers that support mouse click (easy) and query type-in (flexible) interactions (Sections 3.3, 3.4, and 3.5). A natural capability to provide is for type-in boxes to accept natural language input and process such inputs in a reasonable way. The keyword and n-gram based matching algorithms that we have used generally meet this requirement. Web search engines have often relied on boolean functions of keywords implicitly or explicitly expressed in the query. This often does not result in reasonable behavior in response to natural language queries. Vector based approaches tend to be more robust with respect to natural language queries because partial matches are easily calculated, whereas boolean functions applied simply either match fully or not at all. However, Search engines now generally accept natural language queries and treat them in a reasonable manner. Some even recommend natural language queries. An easy way to use vectors for natural language queries and boolean expressions for other queries is to simply determine whether the query meets a specified syntax for boolean expressions, and if not, use a vector approach. Sophisticated natural language understand is an obvious but currently intractable approach to this problem. The multibrowser systems we have described contain vector based approaches to keywords and n-grams, and so they handle natural language inputs gracefully. 

Question answering. Reader interaction with texts can benefit from being able to answer user questions. Well known modern question answering systems include FAQ finder (Burke et al. 1997) and closely related systems (e.g. Kulyukin et al. 1998), and MURAX (Kupiec 1993). Systems such as FAQ finder and MURAX are intended for use with texts composed of diverse, fairly independent text segments (e.g. lists of different frequently asked questions (FAQs) and their answers, and sets of encyclopedia articles). In contrast, our focus has been on supporting interaction with coherent bodies of text, as commonly found in a particular document. Question answering can also differ substantially from simply handling natural language queries that end in a question mark. For example Ask Jeeves (askjeeves.com) is a question answering system that takes as input a natural language question, returning a list of related questions from its question database for which it has answers. The user then interacts with the system to develop the question into one which is consistent with the user's needs and to which the system has one or more answers. See also Richardson (2000). This iterative strategy for converging on the best response to the user's needs is also the focus of Pilkington (1992) who addresses detecting and repairing human-computer communication failures.###check relevance again###.

Iterative query refinement. The iterative techniques just mentioned exemplify the strategy of interacting with the user to iteratively converge on the best response. As another class of examples, some search engines allow a user to use the results of one query as input to another query. This can be a convenient way to refine queries that return too many URLs. In the simplest case, this is logically equivalent to ANDing the initial and the subsequent query to get a single query. However, interactive query refinement can go beyond that. For example, the response to a query by Northern Light (2000) includes suggestions for new search terms based on the returned set of URLs. Another way to iteratively refine a query is to allow the user to designate an entire document as the "query," and return a list of similar documents. The capability to search for similar documents is now common in search engines. A variety of techniques for identifying similar documents are possible. For example, a document can be analyzed to extract keywords from it and those keywords used as a query. Identification of similar documents can be complemented by filtering or ranking those documents based on more comprehensive but computationally intensive (e.g. cosine based) comparisons with the query document. Even more sophisticated approaches are possible. The best response is not necessarily stored text; it might be a meta-response intended to elicit further input from the user to in turn enable better retrieval of stored text. Then, a dialogue much like the kind of dialogue that takes place between a reference librarian and a library patron could be the best way for user and system to cooperatively uncover useful information. Dialogue systems go all the way back to Weizenbaum's ELIZA system (1965). Other sophisticated approaches are also possible. For example, database tomography based discovery of connections between literatures (Kostoff ###) appears well suited to extension to the case of cooperatively helping the user discover implicit knowledge within a single body of text. 

Ambiguous search terms. Sometimes a search term is polysemous (its meaning is ambiguous). Where it appears, other words in its proximity can usually disambiguate it. For example, as just described Northern Light suggests possible such terms. Berleant 1995 reviews the literature on this; see also more recent work by Berleant (199?###)  and Lager (199?###). By augmenting a query term with properly chosen cooccuring auxiliary terms that serve as meaning indicators for the query term, a query can be augmented to improve the information retrieval precision. Auxiliary terms can be ANDed into the query either by requiring their presence or requiring their absence. For example, a query for documents on banks might require the auxiliary term "river" and require the absence of such terms as "money," "institution," "race," and "track." Since polysemous terms usually occur in only one of their meanings in a particular document (Yarowsky 199###), filtering out documents in which a term is used in an undesired meaning is usually safe and will not filter out documents that are actually wanted. For multibrowser systems, support for using auxiliary disambiguating terms (or manual use of them without explicit support) would be useful in cases where a body of text containing a number of documents is being browsed, as in the Web Alcove Multibrowser, Section 3.5.

Boolean style expressions. A Boolean combination of search terms may be used for retrieval in many search engines. Boolean expressions provide, for example, a mechanism for disambiguating polysemous search terms as just discussed. They provide more control to the user than vector based retrieval, though because a document either matches a Boolean keyword expression or not, it lacks the graduated matching characteristic of vector based retrieval. Ranking may be made on the basis of other characteristics when retrieval is Boolean based, however. Though usually simple and intuitive to professionals with academic degrees in computing, Boolean expressions are not so to all users, as indicated by the need to teach Boolean algebra explicitly in university courses. This is doubtless why most search engines do not require boolean search expressions even when they are used for retrieval. Google.com, for example, automatically ANDs the search terms typed in by the user. Most other search engines allow users to specify search expressions that explicitly use AND and OR operations, either by typing them in or by filling out appropriate forms. Usually this is via an "advanced" search interface. In addition to AND and OR, some search engines support NEAR, which is not a Boolean operator but is useful. Lycos additionally supports adj (adjacent), far (opposite of near), and before. 

Document ranking. Search engines usually rank the list of returned URLs in order of their estimated value to the user. However, the multibrowsers we have discussed do not rank the different subwindows. Nevertheless, rankings are useful because they help the user prioritize among options - they provide an HCI benefit of reducing the cognitive overhead of choosing. 

Ranking is easier to do when the retrieved items are all retrieved using the same retrieval algorithm than if different algorithms are used. Yet using different algorithms is arguably more robust since any limitations in one algorithm that causes relevant items to fall through the cracks are likely to be compensated for in other algorithms (Soans master's thesis, year?###). However the multiple algorithm approach has the additional potential problem of being more time consuming, since more than one algorithm would need to be run. As a consequence of the advantages of using one algorithm, that is the approach usually used in Web search engines. While multibrowsers have used multiple algorithms, as in the Standalone Multibrowser, we have gradually moved away from that. Subsequent multibrowsers allowed some user choice in algorithm, but a single algorithm was used to process any given query. Unfortunately, rankings can be deceptive, as the quality of the ranking may be low, a problem often mentioned as a deficiency of Web search engines. Search engines usually give few details about their ranking algorithms, nor do they usually comment usefully on their quality (for example, an on-line help page at one leading search engine simply states that "the most relevant content will always appear at the top of the result pages."). A partial exception to the rule is Lycos, which explains its ranking algorithm and even provides an interactive interface to help the user customize the algorithm to the current situation. Goto does not explain its algorithm in detail, but does allow the user to control the importance of a query term by giving it a coefficient ("2.0*lakes" makes the term "lakes" more important). Google endeavors to make good rankings by taking into account numerous factors, such as number of incoming links and nearness of the search terms in  retrieved documents. Many search engines return a numerical relevancy rating, giving the impression of measuring document relevance, but it is not clear what the numbers mean or if indeed they mean anything. Consequently, qualitative rankings that order returned items without numerically rating them would be easier to justify.

The uncertain quality of Search engine rankings is one important motivation for multibrowser systems that work on sets of Web documents, such as our Web Alcove Multibrowser (Section 3.###). Web search engine users can benefit from advanced interfaces to help them deal with the long, poorly ordered lists of URLs that these systems often return. The problems with search engine ranking algorithms are exacerbated by the fact that a cottage industry has grown up around understanding the ranking algorithms of major search engines, to facilitate composing Web pages that will rank well ([7][11][12]###what were they??). Good rankings give commercial interests a competitive edge by bringing their Web pages to the attention of potential customers ahead of competitors' pages. Furthermore some search engines are now selling ranking priority. That is, their ranking algorithms are strongly affected by payments made by sites in returned lists! Because the contribution of the intrinsic interest of these pages to their rankings, uncertain to begin with, can be circumvented significantly by these methods, advanced browsing approaches like that proposed here are even more important.

Search term generalization. Often, variant forms of a keyword (plurals, synonyms, etc.) are useful for retrieval although it may be inconvenient to explicitly put them all in a query. Here we review three ways in which Web search engines commonly handle this. Multibrowsers could benefit by using the same techniques. 

  1. Wildcards. When wildcards are available, a user will typically be allowed to specify the query term "comput*" to retrieve documents containing terms beginning with the string "comput," such as "computer," "computers," "computation," and "computing." Many of the major search engines provide this capability. Some also allow wild cards at the beginning and in the middle of search terms, and allow either a "*" wild card (which matches any number of unspecified characters) or a "?" or "%" wildcard (which matches exactly one unspecified character). 
  2. Stemming algorithms that allow a search term to match its syntactic variants. It is often reasonable to return text containing the term parachute in response to a query using the term parachutes. This can be done through stemming algorithms. Generic stemming rules reduce various different terms to their respective syntactic common denominators. Idiomatic stemming rules apply to just one or a few terms, such as converting "swam" to "swum." Hotbot is one search engine that has both kinds of stemming rules. A toggle is provided to use them or not. Some search engines try to stem automatically as part of their processing strategy. Stemming can be done well or poorly; a query to one search engine specifying one of our surnames, Berleant, returned URLs relating to comedian Milton Berle.
  3. Semantic generalization methods. Thesauri associate words of similar meaning but dissimilar form. Retrieval algorithms can use thesauri to respond to a query containing the keyword "dog" with texts containing the word "canine," for example. Another way to find associations among related words is with collocation analysis, in which a large body of text is analyzed to determine which words tend to co-occur with which other words. Excite, and its partners Magellan and AOL's Netfind suggest viewing input terms as concepts rather than just keywords, because they can generalize an input query term to other related terms. For example, all three sites state that they associate the concepts "elderly people" and "senior citizens." The application to multibrowsers is along the same lines as to search engines.

4.4 Metaphors and Paradigms for Reader Interaction with Text

The present work investigates direct multidisplay as a paradigm for reader interaction with text. By paradigm we simply mean "a pattern, example, or model" (Webster's 1974), with or without the overarching breadth "paradigm" sometimes seems to suggest. Some paradigms for reader interaction with text can be defined intensionally by listing the defining characteristics, as with direct multidisplay (Section 2). Other paradigms are best characterized with a metaphor, as with electronic books, where the book concept is used metaphorically to describe an approach to reader interaction with text that does not involve actual books at all, yet is distinctly and purposely book-like in important ways. Placing the direct multidisplay paradigm for reader interaction with text in context, this section lists the other main paradigms for reader interaction with text (for an analysis see Berleant 2000).

Hypermedia

Before the Web, hypertext was usually applied to circumscribed bodies of related material, although from the beginning it was envisioned as a way to organize very large collections of information (Bush 1945; Nelson 1995). Hypertext tends to be author intensive, since the author must create not only the text but the links as well although automatically generated links are a continuing topic of investigation. The relatively static nature of hyperlinks (Halasz 1988) can be a disadvantage when the user's information-seeking goals do not match the author's information-presentation goals (Berghel et al. 1999). Hypertext has limited capability to satisfy information exploration needs in large collections of information (like the Web), as the typical rather unstructured networks of texts and links make it easy for users to get lost motivating such useful facilities as directories and the bot-generated indexes called search engines. Differentiation between different types of links is typically via the descriptive labels that the user clicks on. The hypermedia paradigm is a natural part of a multibrowser system because of the usability benefit of navigation by clicking, though the links do not necessarily have to be statically defined during preprocessing.

E-books

The book is one of the oldest and most mature information presentation techniques. Aspects of books that contribute to its usefulness include tables of contents, indexes, sequential page numbers, and chapters. The book concept motivated such electronic browsing systems as SuperBook (Egan et al. 1989), the Book Emulator (Benest 1990), and Lector (Raymond 1992), as well as recent commercial offerings of hand held devices of book size, even including a book-shaped device that opens into two displays (like two pages) around a hinged spine (Everybook, Web site). Adherence to the book metaphor can both benefit system quality by suggesting useful features of books to be incorporated, and inhibit it by implicitly guiding design away from ways computers could improve on books, such as sophisticated retrieval techniques as well as new presentation techniques such as direct multidisplay. Perhaps the future will hold electronic book-like devices which improve upon printed books with new characteristics, such as direct multidisplay.

Newspapers

Newspapers present news articles in a distinctive style. The most important information is presented first in two distinct ways. The most important articles are presented first, and within a particular article the most important information is presented first, such that a reader can stop reading a news article on arbitrary paragraph boundaries and know that what was left unread was not considered as important as what was read. Newspapers also present a number of discourse threads simultaneously, in that a page contains multiple articles. Thus, the newspaper embodies a good presentation strategy for busy readers with short attention spans. The newspaper metaphor as a way to present information electronically is explored by the KRAKATOA Web based system (Kamba et al. 1995) and by Golovchinsky and Chignell (1997).  

Citations  

Citations have historically played a central role in the scholarly world as the mechanism for formally relying on existing foundations when building new material. On the Web, hyperlinks are the newly supported mechanism for referencing other work. Links to other documents can be regarded as citations in cyberspace, though the quality of such citations is uncontrolled (Simonson and Berleant, submitted; Simonson et al. 1998) due to both the relative impermanence of Web content (links can break, and document content can change, unlike for paper documents) and because linking is affected by factors less frequently found in scholarly works (for example, commercial competitors are relatively unlikely to link to each other even when this would be helpful to others, and quality of works is less easily assessed). Nevertheless, the use of hyperlinks on the Web has caused citations to become vastly more widespread than before, when they were largely limited to scholarly works. Even in scholarly works, hyperlinks from a papers reference list to the papers it cites would be a much more convenient implementation for citations than the traditional paper method because a clickable hyperlink is so easy to follow, suggesting that influential digital libraries such as those of ACM and IEEE would benefit users by providing papers (or at least their reference lists) in HTML format. Because of the undependability in the permanence of content on the Web, Web content permanence technologies such as archiving (Kahle 1997), Web based document version retention (Simonson et al. 1998 provides a survey; see also www.webdav.org), URNs (Sollin and Masinter 1994) and PURLs (www.purl.org) take on additional significance as potential cited sources due to the value of reliability in citations.

Composition  

By composition we refer to the incremental creation of a composite presentation from separately useful parts (Golovchinsky and Chignell 1997). Halasz's "browsers" and "fileboxes" (1988) were early examples in which the user interactively builds up a representation, in that case one that abstractly describes a collection of passages. Other examples of the composition paradigm include Elastic Windows (Kandogan and Schneiderman 1997), sticky portals in Pad++ (Schneiderman, Web site), the Krakatoa interactive newspaper system (Kamba et al. 1997), and the CollageMachine (Kerne 1997) Web browser. More mundane applications of the composition paradigm include Web browser bookmark lists and browsers' maintenance of a per-session history list, which may be traversed by back and forward buttons.

An application of the composition paradigm to interactive multidisplays suggests itself. Navigation from one multidisplay screen to another can mean leaving behind not only the less useful items but the more useful ones as well, but if users could conveniently select individual items to be "sticky" so they stay in the display as navigation proceeds, then the selected items could be retained while navigation proceeds. User-selected items would therefore accumulate in the display. For example, consider a Web search engine that returns lists of URLs 10 at a time on separate pages. It could be helpful if the most useful items found on the first page could be made "sticky" such that they are brought forward to the second page and stay present and visible while the user continues to check more items.

Queries

Queries are dynamic and defined by the user, in contrast to typical hyperlinks which are static and defined by the author. Queries can retrieve multiple results, in contrast to typical hyperlinks which point to a single target. Yet hyperlinks have convenient point-and-click access whereas queries are typically typed. The query concept is a broad one. For example, a query can be applied to a hyperlinked structure and take the form of a request for a place in the structure conforming to a characterization of a sought node-and-link substructure, and in Halasz (1988). This has no obvious hyperlink counterpart. Although the hyperlink paradigm helped produce the Web, and information access on the Web was initially mainly by following hyperlinks around, queries (via the search engines) have since become an essential part of it. The Web without these query based search engines would be an impoverished environment in comparison. The relative advantages and disadvantages of queries and hyperlinks make it natural to use both on the Web, and we use both in the multibrowser systems for similar reasons.

Text Mining

As early as 1987 the importance of mining in hypermedia networks was noted (Halasz 1987:358-360). The most visible example of hypermedia mining is probably the Web search engines, which are based on indexes derived by extensive mining of the Web. In addition to simply mining terms, search engines can mine the Web for the incoming links to a page for use in ranking returned lists of URLs (google.com), interactively suggest new terms that the user might add to the query to make it more specific, where those new terms are determined by mining for co-occurrences (www.excite.com, northernlight.com). A non-search engine example of text mining to support readers is the capability of amazon.com to inform the user, given a book of interest, what other books were sold most often to people buying a given book. Web robots that search the Web for useful items conforming to some user interest are other examples of text mining.

Directories

Encyclopedias and other reference works rely on the directory paradigm. Books contain tables of contents, which are directories. Many Web sites provide small directories in a separate frame, while search engine sites often provide large directories serving as jump pages to the Web. Yahoo provides an interesting combination of the directory and query paradigms that allows users to query after moving down some distance into the directory tree. Directories have the attractive property that the "drilling" process requires user effort that is logarithmic in the total number of items cataloged. However they have the less attractive property of imposing a taxonomy that may not be suited to a given information seeking need. To apply the directory paradigm to a multibrowser system, a directory to the body of text could be placed in a subwindow; the multidisplay strategy is well suited to displaying a directory, which should probably contain clickable items, and be "sticky" as described above under composition so that it stays present when navigation actions cause the other subwindows to be refreshed with new material.

5 Conclusion

The value of ever more flexible, intuitive reader interaction with documents has motivated the development of the direct multidisplay paradigm. We have developed a number of multibrowser systems to demonstrate this paradigm using both client- and server-oriented strategies. Desirable features of multibrowsers are those which facilitate reader interaction with the text while minimizing the cognitive overhead of using the interface. Such features include use of links (for easy interaction) as well as type-in boxes (for flexible interaction), ranking of the passages displayed in separate subwindows (to ease deciding which order to read them in), easy access to the context of retrieved passages (e.g. via scroll bars in the subwindows), and use of ordinary Web browsers as a vehicle (to make a multibrowser system accessible and with a familiar look and feel). This work builds on a foundation of collage based systems, especially those focusing on text presentation (Section 4.2). Future work is needed that focuses on developing the direct multidisplay paradigm in the context of specific practical applications, such as help systems and browsing the returned URL lists from Web search engines (currently underway - see Section 3.5). This approach to reading forms a paradigm, direct multidisplay, for reader interaction with text which augments the existing set of paradigms which can be used in designing systems for reader interaction with documents.
