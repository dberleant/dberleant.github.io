Corpus Properties of Protein Interaction

Descriptions in MEDLINE

(long version)

 

D. Berleant*1, J. Ding1, and A.W. Fulmer2

1Iowa State University, Ames, Iowa 50011,  USA

2Procter and Gamble Corp., Cincinnati, Ohio, USA

Abstract

 

Motivation: Work on extracting protein-protein interactions and other interactions from biomedical texts often relies on a relatively meager understanding of the relative degrees of evidence provided by different attributes of their source passages. We empirically investigated a representative corpus derived from MEDLINE to provide background data potentially useful to designers of systems for mining protein interactions from text. The methodology used could also be used to develop analogous results to support mining of other kinds of interactions such as those between proteins and drugs, between drugs and diseases, etc. Mining interaction information of various kinds can support such important tasks as drug targeting, efficient and effective literature searches for researchers, and automated data base construction.

Results: This paper contributes to a better understanding with an empirical investigation of some prominent attributes of relevant passages as they relate to objective of automatic extraction of protein-protein interactions from biomedical texts. The results will provide background and baseline data useful in building and evaluating biomedical information extraction systems.

Availability and supplementary information: The PMIDs in the corpus used is specified in the long version of this paper, which is at http://www.public.iastate.edu/~berleant/research/corpusproperties.pdf. The database derived from the corpus is available on request.

Contact: berleant@iastate.edu.

 

1  Introduction

 

With the emergence in recent years of digitally stored, easily accessed biomedical texts in large quantities, there has been expanding interest in automated mining of these texts for useful biological facts. Techniques potentially analyze texts in units ranging in size from sets of documents down to individual phrases.

 

Starting with sets of documents as the basic unit, Shatkay et al. (2000) assess the functional relatedness of two genes by computing the amount of overlap in two sets of texts, each set related to a different gene. Stapley and Benoit (2000) use that idea to construct a graph from a body of literature for the genes of a particular organism. Each node represents a gene and arc weights represent the computed relatedness between two genes. Iliopoulos et al. (2001) break a set of documents into clusters based on their contained terms, arguing that to the extent a cluster represents a concept, their technique is doing concept discovery.

 

The individual document forms a smaller unit of analysis. Marcotte et al. (2001) analyze documents individually, identifying ones that discuss protein-protein interactions based on the frequency with which they contain members of a set of 83 indicator terms. These were identified through partially automated analysis of a training set of documents which were known a priori to discuss protein-protein interactions.

 

A passage containing a set of contiguous sentences is one level downward from the full document as the unit of analysis. Analysis methods for such passages often include in their scope the problem of anaphora resolution, which is the disambiguation of pronoun and other generic noun occurrences into the nouns they reference. For example Pustejovsky et al. (2002) extract inhibit relationships using an approach that crosses sentence boundaries as needed to resolve anaphoras. Hahn et al. (2002) describe the MEDSYNDICATE system, which resolves anaphora across sentence boundaries using a centering algorithm.

 

The individual sentence is an even smaller unit of analysis. Sentences have a natural appeal because a sentence, syntactically, is a relatively cleanly definable unit and, semantically, is typically constructed to conform to the natural boundaries of a thought or idea. Craven and Kumlien (1999) extract the cellular locations of proteins from individual sentences based on the words in them without reference to the order or other organization of the words. Rindflesch et al. (2000) extract gene-drug relationships from under-specified parses, drawing heavily on the Unified Medical Language System as a knowledge source. Parsing strategies for extracting a rich set of relationships are reported by Friedman et al. (2001), who supplement complete parsing with fallback strategies, and Yakushiji et al. (2001), who supplement complete parsing with a text preprocessing step.

 

The phrase is an even smaller unit than the sentence. Results presented in this report suggest the potential value of phrases from the standpoint of the information retrieval precision metric, because simple term occurrence characteristics, when used to retrieve phrases, can provide a richer “ore” from which to mine protein interactions than is provided by sentences retrieved based on such characteristics. Individual words are even smaller text units than phrases. An active area of research is recognizing and extracting protein names from biomedical texts. Some recent papers appear in PSB (2003).

 

A number of researchers have specifically addressed protein-protein interactions. Blaschke et al. (1999) extract them from phrases containing protein names and an interaction-indicating verb located somewhere between them. Ng and Wong (1999) report using templates some of which do not require the verb to be between the proteins. (Results such as we present in this report are directed toward informing design decisions such as those just noted for Blasche et al. and for Ng and Wong.) Ono et al. (2001) define patterns as simple regular expressions. Sekimizu et al. (1998) use partial parses of sentences to extract interactions between genes and their products. Thomas et al. (2000) use part-of-speech tagging, templates, and coreference resolution. Park et al. (2001) use combinatorial categorial grammar to obtain a detailed parse of text.

 

Future efforts to mine MEDLINE for protein-protein and other interactions are expected as the bioinformatics community organizes benchmark tasks and repositories to support competitive evaluations of bioinformatics text mining systems (DeConde 2002). Statistical approaches to text mining can clearly benefit from baseline statistical properties of the repository, and such approaches become increasingly attractive as the size of the body of text to be processed gets larger. In the biomedical domain, one reasonable objective of a mining project is to process all of MEDLINE, which contains several tens of gigabytes. Such efforts could benefit from knowledge about relevant statistical properties of passages in MEDLINE or other representative repositories. Even systems that attempt a full parsing strategy will sometimes be unable to parse a passage with certainty, and statistical properties potentially provide evidence useful in such cases. Finally, semantic uncertainty can still exist even after a complete and unambiguous parse (consider for instance the sentence “They parked by the bank” which is consistent with both a financial transaction and lunch at the riverside).

 

Statistical results such as those presented here also provide baseline figures from which the performance of other systems can be better gauged. Thus if basic statistical analysis provides a baseline value for some text mining performance measure of 75%, then a 5 percentage point difference in performance between two systems is comparatively larger than it would be if the baseline value was 50%, because 5 percentage points is a greater fraction of the 75-100% range than of the 50-100% range. Also, a baseline performance value can form a minimum value that sophisticated systems presumably would be expected to exceed.

 

2  Design

 

The Protein Interactions in MEDLINE Test (PIMT) Corpus is a body of 303 MEDLINE abstracts chosen because of their relevance to one of ten representative queries to PUBMED. Each query consisted of two protein names elicited from biologists to be representative of queries that biologists might make. Some further details about the PIMT corpus appear in Ding and Berleant (2002); a complete list of the PMIDs of the MEDLINE abstracts in the corpus is given in Appendix A.

 

Each sentence in the corpus mentioning both terms in the query that retrieved its containing abstract or their synonyms was analyzed manually, and its attributes stored in a database. The database facilitated the goal of selecting, ordering, etc., sentences based on configurations of attribute values. These attributes were chosen to illuminate issues relevant to the following questions.

 

1)       How much evidence of an interaction between two proteins is provided by the presence of verbs or other terms suggesting an interaction?

2)       Given two protein names and a verb or other interactor, how does their collective presence within the bounds of a single phrase affect the likelihood of an interaction being described compared to their presence within the bounds of a single sentence?

3)       How does the order of appearance of two protein names and an interactor in a phrase or sentence affect the probability that they describe an interaction?

4)       How does the number of words intervening between two protein names affect the probability that an interaction between them is described?

5)       How does whether two protein names are in a subject-object relationship or not affect the probability that an interaction is described?

6)       How do properties of interactors such as their identity, inflection, root, and semantic category affect the probability that they help describe an interaction between two protein names that are also present?

 

Questions 1-5 are discussed here, while due to length considerations question 6 is deferred to a future report.

 

2.1  Definitions

 

While terms like phrase and sentence are intuitively clear, a study of this type requires definitions that are stated in a computable way, because its results are intended for computer automated text mining. Occasional lack of consistency between such a definition and human judgement is less important than computability and, because we are interested in baseline results that can help support research on more sophisticated algorithms, they should be relatively simple as well. The major definitions are given next, using boldface where they occur. These terms will be used later as needed to describe the database and findings.

 

Sentence

1)       A character in {. ? ! "} followed by whitespace followed by a character in {<capital letter>, <digit>, (, [, {, <, ", '} unless that would create a sentence ending in “i.e.” or “e.g.” is a sentence.

2)       An article title is a sentence.

3)       The <AbstractText> and </AbstractText> XML tags in MEDLINE respectively begin and end sentences. This ensures that the first and last sentences of an abstract will be recognized.

4)       Only sentences containing a co-occurrence of query terms or their synonyms were considered.

Phrase

1)       The beginning or end of a sentence is also the beginning or end of a phrase.

2)       {,  ;  :} each indicate the end of one phrase and the beginning of the next.

3)       <whitespace> – <whitespace> (a dash or hyphen with whitespace on each side) indicates the end of one phrase and the beginning of the next.

4)       Left and right parentheses indicate the start and end of a phrase.

5)       Only phrases containing a co-occurrence of query terms or their synomyms were considered.

 

Query term occurrence

1)       A query term that is not followed by the word “channel,” “receptor,” or “like,” and is not hyphenated with “-like,” is a query term occurrence.

2)       A synonym of a query term that is not followed by the word “channel,” “receptor,” or “like,” and is not hyphenated with “-like” is a query term occurrence.

Note that references to the gene for a given protein are not ruled out by this definition.

Phrase co-occurrence

 

Recall that each abstract in the corpus contains both terms of a query. If a query term occurrence of one query term and a query term occurrence of the other are both present in the same phrase, this is defined to be a phrase co-occurrence. Note that if a phrase contains m query term occurrences of one query term and n of the other, then it contains n*m phrase co-occurrences.

 

Sentence co-occurrence

 

A sentence co-occurrence is defined like a phrase co-occurrence except that the text unit under consideration is a sentence instead of a phrase. Since a phrase is in a sentence, a phrase co-occurrence is also a sentence co-occurrence.

 

Interaction

 

If one query term occurrence affects the state of another, or affects the activity of another on some other chemical or process, there is considered to be an interaction between the two.

 

Admissible sentence triple

 

If a sentence contains a sentence co-occurrence and also an interactor (defined soon), let those three terms be called a sentence triple. A sentence triple in which the interactor is in the same phrase as at least one of the query term occurrences is called an admissible sentence triple. Characteristics of admissible sentence triples were analyzed in this study (sentence triples were not).

 

Phrase triple

 

If all three components of an admissible sentence triple are in the same phrase, they are also a phrase triple.

 

Interactor

 

A verb or some other word or word sequence can indicate an interaction between proteins, and was counted as an interactor if it was part of at least  admissible sentence triple encountered in the corpus. A complete list of interactors found appears in Appendix B.

 

The terms defined in this section will be italicized the first times they appear in the sequel for clarity.

 

3  Data Analyses

 

In this section we address the questions mentioned earlier in Section 2. As stated, the questions are somewhat vague, so we use them to motivate related questions stated using the comparatively precise terms defined above, and then answer those questions based on the database generated from manual analysis of the corpus. The first few uses of the terms defined earlier will be italicized to emphasize that fact. We start by discussing issues related to the presence, absence, and quantity of interactors next in section 3.1, followed by a few comments on phrases vs. sentences in section 3.2. Effects of the number of words intervening between co-occurring protein names are discussed in section 3.3, which also discusses the order of the three terms (protein names and interactor) and their part-of-speech relationship.

 

3.1  Presence, absence, and number of interactors

 

Let us begin with a basic question: given a sentence co-occurrence, is it more likely to indicate an interaction when there is an interactor present? To answer this we determine, given a sentence that contains two protein names, if their presence in an admissible sentence triple enhances the probability that the sentence describes an interaction between those proteins.

 

To that end, we counted 486 sentences with one or more sentence co-occurrence(s), and 644 sentence co-occurrences overall. Of these, only 35 had co-occurrence(s) not in any admissible sentence triple (that is, with no candidate interactor present). These 35 sentences had one sentence co-occurrence each, except for one which had four, totaling the 38 shown in Table 1. As Table 1 also shows, sentence co-occurrences that are part of one (or more) admissible sentence triples have a considerably higher probability of describing an interaction (p<.001, χ2 test) than those that are not. However, because of the low percentage of co-occurrences that were not in any admissible sentence triple, the overall effect of using all sentence co-occurrences vs. using only those in admissible sentence triples for mining protein interactions is modest, increasing precision from 52% to 55% at the cost of retrieving “only” 0.99 of the interactions otherwise retrieved, resulting in an increase in the effectiveness metric (e.g. Baeza-Yates 1999) from 0.68 to 0.71 (2.6 percentage points or 3.8%) relative to interactions occurring in all sentence co-occurrences. An example of one of the few sentences containing an interaction described without an interactor follows.

 

Sentence 80 (in which phospholipase C affects insulin, but no interactor states this):

“The inability to activate a nutrient- and calcium-regulated phospholipase C isozyme in mouse islets to the same extent as in rat islets appears to account, at least in part, for these different insulin secretory responses under these unique conditions.”

 

A similar analysis was done for phrases to answer the following question: given a phrase co-occurrence, is it more likely to indicate an interaction when there is an interactor present? To answer it we determine, given a phrase containing two protein names, if their presence in an phrase triple enhances the probability that the phrase describes an interaction between those proteins.

 

There were 312 sentences with one or more phrase co-occurrences, and because 2 sentences each contained 2 phrases with co-occurrences, there were 314 separate phrases with co-occurrences. However because multiple phrase co-occurrences can occur in one phrase (Section 2.1), there were 351 phrase co-occurrences in the corpus.

 

As Table 1 shows, phrase co-occurrences that are part of one (or more) phrase triples have a considerably higher probability of describing an interaction (p<.001, χ2 test) than those that are not. However, because only 4.8% of phrase co-occurrences were not in any phrase triple, the overall effect of using all phrase co-occurrences vs. using only those in phrase triples for mining protein interactions is modest, increasing precision from 67% to 71% (3.4 percentage points or 5.1%).

The lower precisions of triples compared to co-occurrences is because for a given co-occurrence there are often multiple interactors (and therefore multiple triples) present, and generally no more than one describes an interaction between the co-occurring protein names.

 

To better understand answer this issue, we first broke out sentences and phrases into categories depending on how many co-occurrences they had, and analyzed the categories individually. The percentage of examples in each category is shown in Figure 1. The preponderance of passages had one co-occurrence, and one-co-occurrence passages are likely to be easier to automatically analyze than those with more than one co-occurrence. These facts make one-co-occurrence passages of particular interest in system design, and so this category is examined in some detail next.

Figure 1. Of phrases, 91% had only one co-occurrence, while only 7.6% had two and other numbers had less than 1%. Sentences were more heterogeneous, with a lower percentage having one co-occurrence than for phrases (77%) and higher percentages having other numbers of co-occurrences.

 

Effects of the number of interactors

 

The 415 out of 486 (77%) sentences and 285 out of 315 (90%) phases with one co-occurrence were further categorized by the number of triples associated with each co-occurrence (i.e., the number of interactors associated, by virtue of the definitions in Section 2.1, with that co-occurrence). Figure 2 presents some results. The peaks show that a higher percentage of co-occurrences were in 2 triples than any other number of triples. This indicates that text analysis techniques can benefit greatly by being able to distinguish between interactors that indicate an interaction between a given pair of proteins and interactors that are just in the vicinity (though they may indicate an interaction between other proteins).

 

Figure 2. Percentage of co-occurrences associated with 0, 1,… triples, for 1-co-occurrence sentences and phrases.

 

Apparently, many sentences and phrases contain one co-occurrence but multiple interactors, many of which must therefore be uninvolved in describing an interaction between the co-occurring pair of proteins. They might, for example, describe interactions between other nouns, as in the following example:

 

Sentence 47 (describing an inhibition relationship between a query term co-occurrence of insulin and PLC): “Enhanced phosphoinositide hydrolysis via overexpression of phospholipase C beta1 or delta1 inhibits stimulus-induced insulin release in insulinoma MIN6 cells.”

 

Techniques for extracting information about interactions may vary depending on the complexity of the sentence or phrase, and one way to describe that complexity is by the number of triples (and hence interactors) associated with a given co-occurrence. Therefore, we determined:

1)       the percentages of interactions associated with co-occurrences in 1 triple (and are therefore relatively simple), in 2 triples (and therefore less simple), in 3 triples, etc.; and

2)       the percentages of 1-co-occurrence sentences and phrases describing interaction(s) and containing 1, 2, 3, etc., triples.

These two curves are not identical because a given co-occurrence may help describe two interactions, thereby counting twice in 1) but only once in 2). However if they were similar it would certainly make things conceptually easier. Figure 3 shows that they are similar, as well as qualitatively similar to the results shown in Figure 2. While perhaps not surprising this is nonetheless comforting since these similarities are not logically required. Thus Figure 3 verifies what Figure 2 suggests, that being able to distinguish interactors that indicate an interaction between a given protein pair from other interactors present is a valuable characteristic of a protein interaction extraction technique.

 

Figure 3. For both sentences (Ss) and phrases (Ps) containing 1 co-occurrence (the large majority –  see Figure 1), a higher percentage of interactions occurred in passages containing 2 triples than in passages containing other numbers of triples. Similarly a higher percentage of passages describing interactions between a given co-occurrence contained 2 triples than other numbers of triples. This is consistent with Figure 2. All four curves are qualitatively similar.

 

So far, we have not discussed the likelihood that an interaction between co-occurring protein names is described as a function of the number of interactors associated with the co-occurrence (that is, the number of triples containing the co-occurrence). Such information could be of significant value to a mining system. To address this, we examined the data with respect to the two following specific issues.

1)       Since a given interaction often will need only one interactor in its description (and hence only one triple), we might expect the likelihood that a specific triple describes the interaction to be lower when more triples are present, since only one of them will be relevant. This turns out to be the case for both sentences and phrases (Figure 4).

2)       One might wonder if the presence of more interactors tends to increase the likelihood that one of them is used in an interaction description. This appears not to be the case. In fact, Figure 4 shows that the likelihood of a given co-occurrence being in an interaction description is relatively steady over a range from 1 to 5 triples, for both sentences and phrases. Co-occurrences with very large numbers of triples, 6 or more, were relatively unusual (24 1-co-occurrence sentences, or 6.4%, and 10 1-co-occurrence phrases, or 3.5%). An example of one such outlying passage is:

 

Sentence 121 (describes interactions, but not between query term occurrences gibberellin and amylase): “In addition, abscisic acid-mediated inhibition of gibberellin-stimulated responses seems to depend on the activation of a phospholipase D during induction of alpha-amylase in barley aleurone cells as well as on a putative acetyltransferase involved in elongation growth.”

 

Figure 4. Some results regarding 1-co-occurrence passages. These comprise most of the passages (see Figure 1). The lower two curves show that the probability of an interactor helping to describe an interaction between given query term occurrences in its proximity decreases the more interactors are present. The upper two curves show that the probability of a passage describing an interaction are relatively unaffected by the number of interactors present within the range 1-5, which is most of them (recall Figure 2). For 1-co-occurrence passages overall, 62% of the sentences and 70% of the phrases (68% and 74% respectively when 0-triple passages were excluded) described an interaction between the query term occurrences (cf. Table 1 which tabulates analogous data for co-occurrences, not passages, and includes passages with other numbers of co-occurrences as well).

3.2 Comparing sentences and phrases

 

For co-occurrences that are in a triple (rows 1 & 4 in Table 1), phrases have a sizeable advantage over sentences in precision, 0.71 vs. 0.55, while sentences allow retrieval of more interactions, 334 vs. 236. However, the value of the information retrieval recall metric R (from 0 to 1) depends on the total number of interactions, some of which are described over text units larger than sentences. The information retrieval effectiveness metric E weights recall R and precision P equally using the formula E=2RP/(R+P). Let Rs be the recall for set S, defined to contain sentence co-occurrences that are in at least one admissible sentence triple. The effectiveness of set P, defined to contain phrase co-occurrences that are in at least one phrase triple, exceeds the effectiveness of S only if Rs>0.965.

 

For co-occurrences that might not be in a triple (rows 3 & 6 in Table 1), similar observations apply, except that the effectiveness of phrase co-occurrences cannot exceed that of sentence co-occurrences. Since Rs is lower than 0.965 (Ding et al. 2002), sentences as the unit of analysis rate higher in effectiveness than phrases, though not by much, and for the precision metric phrases are higher.

 

3.3  Co-occurrence separation, term order, and subject-object relationship

 

Every co-occurrence of protein names may be characterized by the separation of the two terms. It is reasonable to ask how the degree of separation affects the number of co-occurrences and interactions that may be found. Automatic text analysis techniques can use such results as one source of information to guide analysis and fact extraction.

 

Figure 5 compares the sizes of the set of sentence co-occurrences and three pertinent subsets, as functions of the number of words separating the co-occurring terms. The separation can be zero when the terms are next to each other or when intervening material is hyphen-connected (“…A-induced B…” has zero words between A and B).

 

 

Figure 5. The cumulative sentence co-occurrences curve rises rapidly toward the total of 644 in the corpus before slowing its rise and becoming nearly level for large separations, with the largest number of intervening words of any sentence being 56.  Requiring the interactor to be one of the intervening words reduces the height of the curve by about 25% in the right half of the graph, and somewhat higher percentages in the left half. This is also true for the two curves for phrases, but these level off sooner since phrases tend to be shorter than sentences.  

 

Text patterns might be expected to affect the quality of co-occurrences as sources of information about interactions. For example, if an interactor is between the two terms in a co-occurrence, is the resultant triple more likely to indicate an interaction description than if the interactor is before or after the co-occurrence? Similarly, does the fact that the terms in a co-occurrence have a subject-object relationship or not affect the likelihood that there is a description of an interaction? We examine each of these pattern questions in turn.

 

Effects of whether the interactor is between the co-occurring terms

 

We have previously described how limiting attention to co-occurrences which have an interactor between the co-occurring terms reduces the number of co-occurrences under consideration (Figure 5). This seems undesirable. However, if the richness of the resulting subset of co-occurrences as a source of interactions to extract (that is, its precision) is higher than the precision of its containing set, then this would be desirable. This in fact turns out to be the case (see Figure 6). These facts underline the need for system designers in this area to make design decisions reflecting the classic recall vs. precision information retrieval tradeoff.

 

Figure 6. Cumulative precisions of a number of kinds of co-occurrences as functions of the  number of intervening words. For smaller numbers of intervening words, having the interactor occur between the co-occurring terms tends to give relatively high precisions for both phrase and sentence co-occurrences. The set of sentence co-occurrences includes phrase co-occurrences, however a closer look at just those sentence co-occurrences that are not also phrase co-occurrences tells a different story. This perhaps oddly defined set shows low precision in general, and dramatically low precision for low numbers of intervening words. This shows that when an interaction is described over a modest number of words, it will usually be in a phrase, and that for non-phrase co-occurrences with few intervening words, the fact that co-occurring terms are in separate phrases indicates that an interaction between them is not described. For larger numbers of intervening words all the precisions decrease, showing that the degree of separation of co-occuring terms is negatively associated with the likelihood that an interaction between them is described. The moderate degree to which the curves tend downward in the right hand area of the graph is due mostly to the relatively small numbers of examples they add to the cumulation relative to the examples with smaller separations already present.

 

 

 

Figure 7. Precisions of four categories of co-occurrences as functions of their separation. Their cumulative forms were shown in Figure 6, while non-cumulative forms are shown here. Due to some quite small numbers of examples of particular categories of co-occurrence at particular separations, especially for larger separations, the plots are noisy. Nevertheless these plots show a marked tendency to decrease in precision as separation increases.

 

Effects of subject-object relationship

 

Most interactions were described using one protein name as the subject and the other as the object of some verb phrase. However, a significant minority were described with other syntactic structures. Like for other effects discussed earlier, subject-object effects might be suspected of depending in some way on whether the interaction is described within the bounds of a phrase, or instead, across phrase boundaries. A system designer might also want to know if passages containing interaction descriptions, co-occurrences helping to describe interactions, and triples helping to describe interactions might differ in some way with respect to subject-object effects. We quantified these issues to provide additional guidance in system building. Such a quantification can help answer questions regarding such issues as the benefit achievable by extracting interactions in which the proteins are not in subject-object syntactic relationships, and the relative advantages in this context of accounting for protein names using triples, co-occurrences, or passages.

 

Table 4 tabulates interactions occurring in phrases, across phrasal boundaries, in subject-object relationships, and not in subject-object relationships. The various figures in that table show a strong tendency for protein relationship descriptions to use subject-object syntax within a phrase: over 60% of the relationship descriptions were in that category, several times the remaining <10% found in phrases and over four times the amount found in the two across-phrase categories.  Interestingly, for relationships described across phrase boundaries, unlike for those described within phrases, about half used subject-object syntax and half did not. Overall, nearly ¾ of the relationships were described within phrases, and just over ¾ were described using subject-object syntax.

 

The data in Table 4 deserves a few additional comments. Relative recalls were fairly similar whether tabulated for triples, co-occurrences, or sentences. The fact that every co-occurrence is in a triple and every triple is in a sentence only explains this in part, because these relationships are often n-to-1 as indicated by the different numbers of total triples, co-occurrences, and sentences. 4  Conclusion

 

Analysis showed that most protein name co-occurrences are accompanied by words that can indicate interactions, but only 22% of triples containing two given protein names and an interaction term actually do describe an interaction. The median number of words intervening between co-occurring protein names was 7, although a few had up to a few dozen intervening words. Requiring that the interaction term be one of the intervening words reduced recall to 78% of the interactions retrieved without this restriction, while dramatically improving precision (of triples) by a factor of 3.7. Limiting attention to phrases reduced the number of interactions retrievable to 72% of the interactions retrievable using full sentences. However, interactions were described for 68% of the protein co-occurrences in phrases but only 52% of those in sentences overall (which includes phrases). Most interactions between protein name co-occurrences were described using a subject-object syntactic relationship between the names, 77%, vs. only 23% for the others.
